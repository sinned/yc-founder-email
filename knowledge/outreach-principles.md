# Founder / Startup Outreach Principles

Durable principles for cold(ish) outreach from this repo. Distilled from building the
YC batch email. Apply these to any future founder/startup outreach here, not just the
current draft. When a principle and a one-off edit conflict, the principle wins unless
there's a stated reason.

Audience assumption: busy, skeptical founders (YC batch and similar) who get pitched
constantly. Sender is **Dennis Yang, 3x founder, now at Anthropic.** The peer-founder
identity is the core asset; protect it in every line.

---

## 1. Opens are won by the sender first, then the preview, then the subject

- The from-line does heavy lifting. `Dennis Yang <...@anthropic.com>` already supplies
  brand pull and curiosity. So the **subject's job is not to re-announce the brand**,
  it's to signal *a real human / peer, not a campaign.*
- **Inbox preview text is the second half of the open decision.** Line 1 of the body
  shows next to the subject. So a plain, human subject + a sharp, specific first line
  beats a clever subject with a generic preview. Front-load the most specific, personal
  words into the opening line (in templated sends, the `[PERSONAL HOOK]`).

## 2. Subject lines: legitimacy-led, peer, no "official"

- Current winner: **`a note from a 3x founder at Anthropic`**.
- "real human / peer / a note" framing IS the legitimacy signal. It says *not an
  automated blast.* That disarms the skeptic better than credentials.
- **Avoid the word "official"** and press-release phrasing. For this audience it reads
  as vendor blast, which is the one thing we must not be.
- Watch ambiguity: "a founder at Anthropic" can be misread as *a founder OF Anthropic.*
  "3x founder" avoids that and signals an active track record (not "ex-founder," which
  carries an out-of-the-arena, lower-status read to a current founder).
- Subjects can be legitimacy-led OR thesis-led. If legitimacy-led (current choice), the
  **body's first line + hook must carry the argument cold** since the subject no longer
  states it.

## 3. Show the problem, don't tell it

- Drop the founder into a **scene they've lived**, in second person, specific to their
  world. YC example that landed: *"a partner asks what you've actually learned, and
  you're scrolling three weeks of call notes trying to remember who said the thing that
  mattered."*
- Specificity + recognizable stakes + the "that's me" feeling beats any abstract thesis
  ("most of what they tell you evaporates" is telling; the scene is showing).
- **Describing the scene precisely is itself the credibility flex.** "I've done this
  three times" + an accurate scene proves you've been in the seat, which is stronger
  than stating a résumé ("I've started three companies").

## 4. Name the real cost, not the surface symptom

- The pain isn't *forgetting* what users said. It's that the learning **never changes a
  decision.** Name that cost ("evaporates before it changes a single decision" / "acting
  on it before it's gone"). It also hands the baton straight to the solution.
- This sets up the talking-is-easy / *acting-on-it*-is-the-hard-half contrast that is
  the through-line of the whole pitch.

## 5. Lead with the durable payoff (the corpus), not a feature list

- The headline value of found-with-claude is the **durable knowledge corpus** that
  accumulates run after run, not the count of skills. Mention skills briefly and
  parenthetically; give the emphasis to the corpus.
- Keep the punchline **"receipts, not vibes."** Don't dilute it by repeating "receipts"
  elsewhere in the same stretch of copy.

## 6. Peer, never vendor

- The 3x-founder credibility is load-bearing. Every edit must keep the email reading as
  a peer reaching out, not a company marketing. No corporate "we're excited to," no
  feature dumps, no hype adjectives.

## 7. Tighten hard, but don't cut the words that carry stakes

- Target a short body (~120-130 words). Kill darlings: the weak "worked examples are
  inside" line got cut because it whispered where it should have proven.
- But tightening that removes **specificity** removes punch. Cutting "the right call on
  what to build" down to "the right call" made the payoff abstract. Keep the concrete
  noun even when trimming.

## 8. The hook must be true and specific, or cut

- One sentence, genuinely specific (a mutual, their actual launch, a real detail of
  their background). **If you can't verify a real hook, cut the line.** A clean no-hook
  email beats a fake or generic one.
- Generic-line test: *could this sentence be pasted into another founder's email
  unchanged?* If yes, it's generic. Cut it. ("Congrats on the launch!" fails. "We both
  overlapped with Priya at Stripe" passes.)

## 9. Make the ask concrete and low-friction

- Not "would love your thoughts." Yes: *"point one skill at something real and tell me
  what was useful and what broke."* One specific, bounded action.
- Invite honesty over politeness ("I'd take brutal over polite any day"); it raises the
  quality of replies and reinforces the peer tone.

## 10. House style

- **No em-dashes, ever.** Re-scan output before finishing.
- Markdown intended to paste into Google Docs (bold/headings/rules render; code block
  stays monospace).
- Header styled like a real email: To / From / Subject + horizontal rule.
